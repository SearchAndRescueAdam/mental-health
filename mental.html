Mission:
In an effort to combat mental illness on college campuses, we strive to inform students about mental illnesses. By recognizing and identifying ways to offer support to people in need, we can lower the stigma regarding mental health and encourage all to seek professional help.

What Are Mental Health Issues?

Mental Health Issues, refer to a wide range of mental health conditions. These disorders can affect a person's mood, thought, and behavior. Common mental illnesses include anxiety, depression, eating disorders, and addictive behaviors. It is normal for people to face challenges with their mental health from time to time. However, when mental illness continues and no treatment is sought, an individual's ability to function in the community will decline.
“Mental Illness.” Mayo Clinic, Mayo Foundation for Medical Education and Research, 8 June 2019.

What Is Anxiety?

You might feel anxious when faced with a stressful problem at work, before taking a huge test, or before making an important life decision. Everyone experiences common stress; however, there are some that are afflicted with a more chronic and serious form of stress specifically called “anxiety”. Anxiety has the power to take away a person's life, and drive a person to the edge, making it unbearable to live. What effects does anxiety have? Here are some examples from a study by the National Institute of Mental Health.

  • Feeling restless, wound-up, or on-edge
  • Being easily fatigued
  • Having difficulty concentrating, Mind going blank, Being irritable
  • Having muscle tension
  • Difficulty controlling feelings of worry
  • Having sleep problems, such as difficulty falling or staying asleep, restlessness, or unsatisfying sleep

What is Depression?

Depression is a serious mood disorder that affects how you feel about, think about, and handle daily activities. Any case of depression that lasts longer than a series of two weeks would be considered suffering from deep depression. Depression can happen at any age and affects its host with loss of interest and decreased energy and fatigue. “Search.” National Institute of Mental Health, U.S. Department of Health and Human Services.

Common Signs

Anxiety

  • Frequent headaches
  • Nausea
  • Twitches or tremors
  • Excessive sweating
  • Restlessness

Depression

  • Changes in appetite
  • Lack of energy
  • Loss of interest in regular activities
  • Irritable
  • Tearfulness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Dramatic changes in sleeping habits (insomnia/sleeping all the time)
  • Frequent/reoccurring thoughts of suicide

How to help

DO:

  • Validate and take their feelings seriously
  • Commiserate, but don’t say that you “understand”
  • Let them know about other support services (counseling groups, doctors, medication)
  • Respond to emergencies
  • Take care of yourself first
  • Stay aware of resources in your area so you can share information

DON'T:

  • Tell them to “snap out of it”
  • Encourage unhealthy coping habits (drugs/alcohol)
  • Avoid them after they open up to you
  • Judge or criticize
  • Assume the problem will resolve itself
  • Imply that they are a burden
